0. Ground rules this plan is built against


Capacity ceiling: one concurrent Teardown through October. Sign 2–3 in the window, deliver no more than 2. "Signed, scheduled for the November slot" is a selling position, stated as scarcity, never apologized for. Being ahead on the build (Phase 1 week 3 done, weeks 4–5 this weekend) advances the earliest kickoff date, not the ceiling.
Human checkpoint: every named target is approved by Renee before first touch. Research steps below produce candidate lists for approval; nothing goes out unapproved.
No automated outreach. Every first touch is researched, individual, and consultative. Volume discipline: maximum 3 cold first-touches per week, because each one carries 30–45 minutes of research and the research is the product sample.
Pricing discipline: $12,500 published everywhere from day one. The $7,500 pilot exists once, for one direct client, framed as founding-client pricing with a named end date. Channel-referred clients never see a discount — discounting through a partner resets their clients' anchor and devalues the partner's own referral.
Pre-sale research rule (from the methodology): the 2–3 gaps surfaced in research are hypotheses, presented as questions worth answering — never as findings. This is both the liability rule and, it turns out, the better sales move.

2. Channel economics — two lanes, one rate card

The structural principle: the split follows the work, not the relationship. Two lanes, defined by role, decided per deal at introduction time:

LanePartner doesFiremark doesSplitReferralIntroduction + contextSells, delivers, attests, owns the client and the ladder (retainer, packet)15% of first engagement fee, paid on collection. Nothing on renewals, retainers, or subsequent SKUs.SubcontractSold the engagement, owns the client relationship, handles account management, co-leads the readoutDelivers the assessment on the instrument, writes the report, signs the attestationUp to 50/50 when the partner genuinely carries the sale and the client; 60/40 Firemark when Firemark also carries client management and the readout.

Two principled anchors to hold in any negotiation. First, the attestation cannot be split: the signature, the methodology, and the liability are Firemark's in both lanes, which is the floor argument for why delivery-side economics never drop below half. Second, the 15% referral lane is what funds a delivery hire later — a 50% rake on referred deals makes the fungibility model (and the YC math built on it) permanently impossible, so it is not a negotiating position, it is a constraint.

The "split equally" conversation with Evan and Lola

Do not renegotiate the phrase — formalize the structure it was casually pointing at. Both said "split equally any overflow they direct to me": overflow is the subcontract lane, and 50/50 survives there honorably. The move is a one-page rate card presented as clarity-before-the-first-deal, not as a correction:


"Before the first deal lands I want us to have the boring part written down, because I'd rather do it now than mid-engagement. I see two ways clients come through you, and they're worth different things. When you've sold it and it's your client and I'm the delivery arm — the overflow case you mentioned — an equal split is right, and I've written it that way. When it's an introduction and I run the sale, the delivery, and the relationship, the market rate is a 15% referral fee on the first engagement, and that's what lets me eventually put a delivery hire on this instead of capping what we can do together at my personal hours. Every deal, we say which lane it's in when the intro happens. Fair?"



This concedes nothing they were actually promised, honors the 50/50 in the lane where it belongs, and gives the margin-protection reason honestly (hiring capacity) rather than hiding it. Lola's existing structure already is the subcontract lane; for her this is a confirmation, not a change.

Evan: reciprocal referrals instead of fees — yes, with a review clause

Recommended structure for Evan specifically: reciprocal, no fees, for the first two deals in each direction, reviewed at month 6. Reasons it beats a fee structure with him: you are AIGP peers and the deal flow is genuinely two-directional — his clients (companies with employee AI sprawl) need attested assessment, and your Teardown clients' remediation roadmaps regularly prescribe exactly his managed-AI-rollout service, so fees would just churn money back and forth with bookkeeping friction; reciprocity keeps acquisition cost at zero during the cash-tight build months; and peer-to-peer reciprocity builds the kind of relationship that survives into co-marketing (joint webinar, shared content), which a vendor-style fee arrangement doesn't. The risk is imbalance — one side feeds the other and resentment compounds silently. The mitigation is written into the same one-pager: quarterly tally of introductions and closed deals both directions; if the flow is lopsided by more than two closed deals, either side can convert the arrangement to the 15% referral lane, no hard feelings, pre-agreed. Naming the exit ramp up front is what makes the reciprocal arrangement safe to enter.

6. The conversion path

Step 0 — pre-intro research (channel-referred only, and non-negotiable). Before any partner intro call happens, run the 30–45 minute research move on the referred company and arrive with 2–3 specific hypotheses. The partner staked credibility on the introduction; walking in generic spends their capital. Walking in with "I looked at your stack and I'd want answers to these three questions" protects it — and is the single highest-leverage conversion act in this plan.

Step 1 — first touch → discovery call (30 min). Objection: "We're too early for governance." Response, in their own market's vocabulary: it's cheaper to build with compliance than retrofit it — and if you're selling into banks (or providers, or firms), your buyer's diligence team has already decided you're not too early; the only question is whether the answers exist when the questionnaire arrives.

Step 2 — discovery → gap letter. Within 48 hours of the call: a one-page note with the 2–3 hypotheses sharpened by what you heard, framed as the questions a [bank risk team / provider compliance review / firm procurement] will ask, plus the Teardown one-pager (scope, four weeks, $12,500 fixed, what the report contains, the attestation explained in one sentence). The gap letter is the free-research conversion tool doing its real job: proof of specificity, not free consulting — hypotheses stay questions.

Step 3 — proposal → signature. The recurring objections and their answers:


Price vs. Big Four: they'd quote $60–120K and a quarter; this is $12,500, four weeks, and you get the assessor's personal attestation, not a rotating team of associates.
Price vs. software: Vanta-class tools document security compliance; none of them can classify your AI portfolio against AI-specific frameworks, and none of them can sign anything.
"Our lawyer can handle it": counsel interprets law — indispensable and out of my scope; this is a portfolio assessment producing attested classifications and a control gap analysis, which is the input your counsel wants to work from, not a substitute for them.
"Can you just tell us the gaps?" That's the Teardown. The research surfaced hypotheses; assessed findings require evidence, method, and a signature — which is the difference between an opinion and something you can hand your board or your buyer.
"Four weeks is long / we're busy": your team's total time is six to eight hours across the four weeks — one questionnaire session, three to five short interviews, one readout.
Timing/slot pressure: one delivery slot at a time; the contract holds the slot. Scarcity stated once, factually, then dropped.


Step 4 — signature → kickoff inside two weeks, or a named scheduled slot. Day-60 after delivery: the retainer outcome logged, per the methodology's ledger.

Revenue, signed in the window: base case ≈ $18–22K — one Lola subcontract at the 50–60% delivery share of $12,500 (≈$6,250–7,500) plus either the direct pilot at $7,500 or one full-price channel referral at $12,500 less the 15% (≈$10,625). Two honest framing notes. First, this window's real products are not revenue: they are case study #1 with a named reference, the unit-economics ledger's first real rows, and two channel structures formalized in writing — the three inputs the YC application, the hire gate, and the Elevare month-8 evaluation all consume.
