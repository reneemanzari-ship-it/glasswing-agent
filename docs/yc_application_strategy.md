## 3. The Atrium defense

The accusation to pre-empt, in a partner's words: *"Tech-enabled services always says the software will compress delivery costs, and then it doesn't — the software subsidizes human hours instead of replacing them, gross margin never improves, and you've raised venture money to run a consultancy. Atrium."* The defense is not rhetoric; it is a ledger shape.

### Atrium's failure signature (what the ledger must show the opposite of) [VERIFY details in Section 4]

Bespoke, heterogeneous work (every legal matter different, so no unit to compress); expensive professionals hired ahead of proven economics; software spending that grew while human hours per matter stayed flat; price discounting to win deals; no per-engagement instrumentation, so the compression claim was asserted, never measured.

### The five proof points, in evidentiary order

1. **Homogeneity by construction.** The Teardown is a fixed-scope, fixed-price SKU delivered from a versioned methodology with sixteen quality gates. Compression is possible because every engagement is the same shape — this is a design argument available at n=1, and it leads the defense.
2. **Compression with attribution.** The ledger records hours *by stage*, so falling totals decompose into named instrument capabilities: evidence-stage hours fall because extraction automated document review; report-stage hours fall because the narrative agent drafts and the founder edits. "Hours fell because the founder hustled" is Atrium with better vibes; "Stage 2 fell 40% and here is the commit that did it" is the anti-Atrium exhibit.
3. **Fungibility, measured.** Percentage of delivery hours executed (or honestly executable) from the runbook by a non-founder: 65% by design, 70% by month 9, with the judgment core (profile confirmation, review-flag clearance, prioritization, attestation, readout) explicitly and permanently excluded. Atrium never had this number. Having the metric at all is half the defense.
4. **Price integrity and marginal cost.** $12,500 held after the one-time pilot price; zero discounting; marginal API cost per engagement in single-digit dollars from the run ledger. Compression accrues to margin, not to price cuts chasing volume.
5. **Hiring behind the curve, not ahead.** The first operator is hired only when three gates hold (unit-economics targets met on three consecutive engagements, fungibility at 70%, six months of pipeline coverage) — the exact inverse of Atrium's hire-lawyers-then-find-leverage sequence. Post-hire, the kill-shot metric: the hire's engagements at target margin within 90 days.

### The exhibit — the table the ledger must be able to print

| | E1 (pilot) | E2 | E3 | E6 target |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Total delivery hours | ~30–35 | ~26–30 | ≤30 | ≤20 |
| Founder hours | ~20–24 | ~14–16 | ~10–12 | ~7 |
| Runbook-share of hours | ~50–55% | ~60% | ~65% | 70% |
| Gross margin @ $250/hr shadow | pilot price distorts — report honestly | ≥45% | ≥55% | ≥60% |
| API + direct cost | $ actual | $ actual | $ actual | $ actual |
| Stage with largest Δ vs prior | — | named stage + cause | named stage + cause | — |

**At application time (n=1):** show E1 actuals against the methodology's planned hours, the stage-level structure, the fungibility tags, and the E1→E2 partials if engagement two is in flight. State plainly in the answer: "one engagement delivered; here is the instrumentation; here is what the curve must show and when we'll know." Partners forgive early; they don't forgive unmeasured. **At interview time:** two points on the curve. **The discipline this imposes now:** every stage-close hour entry, from the pilot's first day, logged same-day — the application is being written by the ledger starting in August.

## 7. Corporate structure — the attorney brief (schedule the call within two weeks; must be resolved before the pilot contract signs)

**Yes, one attorney conversation covers both** the entity work and the client-contract IP clause — a startup-side business attorney (not a big firm; a solo or boutique who does YC-bound companies) handles the whole list. The combined brief to bring:

**The recommended sequence — statutory conversion, not a new entity.** Convert Firemark LLC (Florida) into a Delaware C-corporation via statutory conversion (Florida and Delaware both permit it). Why conversion over forming a NewCo and assigning assets: the entity is continuous, so the Firemark name, the EIN, any existing contracts, and — critically — the IP travel automatically with no assignment event, no assignment-valuation question, and no orphaned LLC to maintain. The fallback to ask the attorney to rule out: if anything in the LLC's history is worth quarantining (unlikely — it's young and low-liability), form a Delaware C-corp instead and assign IP into it *now, while the instrument's fair value is defensibly low* — the same logic either way: **do this before the pilot delivers and the case study exists, because every week of traction makes the IP transfer question more expensive.**

1. FL LLC → DE C-corp conversion (or NewCo memo if disqualified), including the tax treatment of the conversion for a single-member LLC — ask specifically about the entity's tax posture before and after, and any Florida-side filings.

Founder common stock issued with **four-year vesting, one-year cliff** (yes, on your own company — YC expects it and it protects the company you're building), and the **83(b) election filed within 30 days of issuance** — this deadline is statutory and unforgiving; calendar it the day stock issues.
